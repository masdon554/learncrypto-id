Cryptocurrencies are digital assets that have attracted a lot of attention in recent years. But actually the theory about cryptocurrencies has existed since less than half a century ago and that is where the beginning of Cryptocurrencies was created. Cryptocurrency is not an object that has a physical form (like paper money) and does not have a central authority. Cryptocurrency is binary data designed to work as a medium of exchange where records of individual coin ownership are stored in ledgers that exist in the form of computerized databases that use strong cryptography to create secure transactions, control the creation of additional coins and verify the transfer of coin ownership.

⇒ E-cash, The First Electronic Currency System

The concept of electronic cash (e-cash), or digital currency, is not new. Since the 1980s, e-cash protocols have existed that are based on the model proposed by David Chaum. Just as understanding the concept of distributed systems is necessary to understand blockchain technology, the idea of e-cash is also important to appreciate the first, and very successful, applications of blockchain, Bitcoin and other Altcoins.


The original technical team, from left to right:
Marius, Paul, Branko, Kai, and Mercal.
Source chaum

There are two fundamental problems in the e-cash system that need to be addressed as follows:
  • Accountability is necessary to ensure that cash can only be spent once (overcoming the double-spending problem) and that it can only be used by its rightful owner. The double spending problem arises when the same money can be spent twice. Since it is very easy to make copies of digital data, this is a big problem in digital currencies because you can make many copies with the same amount of digital money.

  • Anonymity is necessary to protect user privacy. With physical cash, it is nearly impossible to trace spending back to the individual who actually paid the money, which provides sufficient privacy if consumers choose to hide their identity. in the digital world, however, providing such a level of privacy is difficult due to the inherent personalization, tracking, and logging mechanisms in digital payment systems such as credit card payments. This is necessary to ensure the safety and security of financial networks, but is also often considered a violation of privacy.

This is due to the fact that end users do not have any control over who their data can be shared with, even without their consent. However, this is a solvable problem and cryptography is used to overcome the problem. Especially in blockchain networks, the privacy and anonymity of participants in the blockchain is a sought after feature. David Chaum solved both of these problems during his work in the 1980s by using two cryptographic operations, namely, Blind Signature and Secret Sharing. For now, suffice it to say that Blind Signature makes it possible to sign a document without actually seeing it, and secret sharing is a concept that allows the detection of double spending, i.e. using the same e-cash token twice.

⇒ DigiCash, The First Electronic Money Company

DigiCash is an implementation of e-cash which is an electronic money company founded by David Chaum in 1989. DigiCash requires a device software for users to pull notes from the bank and assign encrypted keys before they can be sent to recipients. This allows electronic payments to be untraceable and untraceable by the issuing bank, government or third parties. At the time DigiCash was launched, Mercantile Bank was the only United States bank to support the DigiCash system and Deutsche Bank in Germany is the second bank to support the DigiCash system.


DigiCash official website logo. Source Internet Archive.

Special things from DigiCash, as follows:

  • Electronic Transactions, DigiCash was the first serious attempt to create a viable electronic payment system.
  • Anonymous Payments, New cryptographic protocols mean transactions can be sent without users having to divulge any personal information.
  • Security, Chaum's cryptographic tools such as private keys (private keys), also mean that traders can store values secretly in the system.
  • The First Cryptocurrency, DigiCash allows users to send and receive Cyberbucks. A digital currency that is not directly controlled by a centralized institution or bank.

How does DigiCash work?

  • Getting Cyberbucks, DigiCash users first need to withdraw money from their bank account, which will then be converted into Cyberbucks. This will then be saved on the user's PC hard drive.
  • DigiCash Transactions, Users can use DigiCash to pay for goods and services. A payment request will be sent, which once approved, will allow Cyberbucks to be sent over the network to the merchant. Recipients can also automatically convert Cyberbucks into USD dollars that can be deposited directly into their bank account.
  • Blind Signatures, DigiCash transactions can be private. While there are ways for banks to track coins created, users can make transactions in special digital "envelopes." It's a way the bank can ensure the transaction is valid, without the sender having to actually divulge personal information.

The next story is told by Raymond Stofberg who was responsible for DigiCash's financial affairs until August 1996 before DigiCash went bankrupt. Stofberg reached an agreement with Henderson Investment Management. They will invest two tranches totaling $10 million. When Chaum saw the agreement, he immediately faxed it to all the other venture capitalists with whom he was negotiating. Because news leaked to Henderson, the agreement was cancelled. A little later ING Investment Management wanted to do a deal around twenty million guilders."The day we were ready to sign, David didn't want to," says Stofberg.

“He is very paranoid, so he always thinks something is wrong. There were 8 people from ING, including the CEO, and David refused to sign!”.

By Raymond Stofberg, Former CFO of DigiCash

Chaum was previously contacted by Bill Gates from Microsoft. He wanted to integrate cash in every copy of Windows 95. Rumor has it, they were offering something like 100 million dollars. David Chaum refused to sell it. Chaum also terminated a deal with another American company, Netscape, in the same way.

In March 1996, David Chaum had to leave the company due to disagreements between him and employees. He appointed two people, namely Jelte van der Hoek and Wouter Habraken as new directors, then he returned to the US a year later. Soon, the rebels quarreled and most left the company. DigiCash declared bankruptcy in 1998 and later sold its assets including e-cash patents to another digital currency company, eCash Technologies.

In 2000, another company using the domain ecash.com and eCash Technologies sued, alleging trademark infringement and unfair competition. However, the court held that "just because registration of a domain name does not confer trademark rights, let alone "clearly established" rights, ECash Technologies had no obligation to disclose the registration of the domain name "ecash.com", but eCash Technologies later went bankrupt and the domain "ecash. com" remains owned by its original owner.

In 2002 eCash Technologies was acquired by InfoSpace, currently known as Blucora. As of 2015, the term eCash is used for digital money that can be stored on electronically sensitive cards including online or alternative payment portals and mobile applications.

⇒ E-gold, Digital Gold Currency


Kantor G&SR (Operator e-gold). Sumber wikipedia oleh TMatthew22.

E-gold Ltd. is a successful profit service that offers transferable gold denomination accounts. It works in tandem with sister service Gold and Silver Reserve Inc., hereafter G&SR, also known as OmniPay, which sells e-gold units to members of the public in exchange for official money, and buys the units back. Both companies were launched in 1996 by Dr. Douglas Jackson, a Florida oncologist. E-gold also offers silver, platinum, and palladium accounts, but gold accounts ultimately hold 97 percent of the total E-gold account balance based on market value. Jackson said that E-gold was designed specifically for Internet transactions from the start. E-gold holds its account balances in grams of pure gold, and backs them 100 percent with gold bullion held in London. At that time, Brian Grow et al. explained the description of the E-gold model in BusinessWeek in 2006 as follows:

  • Customers can create accounts with fake names (because no one checks them)
  • E-gold can be purchased by Credit Card or Bank Transfer
  • E-gold can be sent between E-gold account users
  • All types of transactions are very convenient and anonymous
But Jackson disputed suggestions that the system was anonymous or that G&SR had no identity controls on E-gold users, although he agreed that such controls were not good enough to block criminal abuse of the system. In his congressional testimony, Jackson explained the benefits of using E-gold, as follows:

  • Ease and low costs for sending money and payments
  • Finality/non-reversibility (cannot be changed)
  • A store of value free from political risk
  • Enables people from any region or economic background to operate globally
  • Access to credit card or bank account
  • E-gold itself is free from chargeback risk

E-gold customers began to grow faster in 2000 and 2001. This was due to competition between companies that were both based on "digital gold currency" payments and provided in-house transfers, such as e-Dinar(2000), e-Bullion (2001), GoldMoney.com(2001) and 3P Pay(2001). Later Douglas Jackson told BusinessWeek that “E-gold has approximately 1.2 million accounts funded through $1.5 billion worth of transactions conducted in 2005” and also said at a congressional hearing “Since its inception, E-Gold has completed more than 67 million individual transactions and currently processes 50,000–70,000 account-to-account transfers per day, worth over $2 billion per year”.

“At E-Gold's peak, the currency will be backed by 3.8 metric tons of gold, worth more than $85 million. E-Gold is now second only to PayPal in the online payments industry.”

Oleh Kim Zettner, Wired 2009

But E-gold's growth ended in December 2005, when FBI and Secret Service agents raided three locations in Florida: G&SR's offices (E-gold Ltd. itself was registered in the Caribbean nation of St. Kitts and Nevis), Jackson's home, and the company's computer servers in Orlando. The FBI discovered credit card network fraudsters were using E-gold accounts to send illicit funds. The justice department then targeted E-gold with the aim of forcing the service to comply with [post-9/11] regulations governing money transfer services. Federal regulations require such businesses to register their business with FinCEN to be licensed in states that require it, diligently authenticate customer identities and file suspicious activity reports on customers who appear shady. But E-Gold doesn't do this. Jackson believes E-gold is exempt from regulation because it is a payment system not a money transfer. He also voluntarily and actively cooperated with federal authorities to provide information about suspicious account activity.

However, the Department of Justice still indicted Douglas Jackson (along with Reid A. Jackson and Barry K. Downey, identified as co-owners of E-gold) in April 2007 on four counts.

"One count of conspiracy to launder monetary instruments, one count of conspiracy to operate a money transmitting business without a license, one count of operating a money transmitting business without a license under federal law, and one count of transmitting money without a license under DC law.”.

Oleh Department of Justice

Arguments that neither E-gold nor G&SR fit the statutory definition of a money transmission business under 18 USC 1960 or 31 USC 5330 were rejected by DC District Court Judge Rosemary M. Collyer. Jackson found that relying on a favorable interpretation of the statutory language was not an effective defense. After moving to dismiss the charges, it was denied, punishable by long prison time and heavy fines. Douglas Jackson and his co-defendants agreed to plea bargains. Jackson was sentenced to 36 months (supervised release), including 6 months of house arrest and electronic monitoring, and 300 hours of community service. He was also forced to hand over about $1.2 million to the government. His company was fined $300,000. The FBI did not seize the gold warehouse (which was in London), but even the most innocent account holders were left without access to their gold.

The justice department gave Jackson the opportunity to reopen the E-Gold and G&SR companies.

“Businesses will establish a comprehensive money laundering detection program that will require verified customer identification, suspicious activity reporting, and regular oversight by the Internal Revenue Services (IRS) Bank Secrecy Act Division, delegated by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network under Federal regulations. E-Gold and G&SR are closed until they can comply”

By Department of Justice

But they never reopened. In December 2010 Jackson announced on the E-Gold blog the approval of a plan to release frozen account balances, which would require account holders to complete an agreed-upon customer identification process. Later in an interview in 2012, Jackson said he was closing E-Gold and G&SR.

⇒ Bit Gold, Decentralized Digital Currency Mechanism Design.

Bit gold was one of the earliest attempts to create a decentralized virtual currency, proposed by blockchain pioneer Nick Szabo in 1998. Although the bit gold project was never implemented, Szabo's efforts were widely considered to be the predecessor to Satoshi Nakamoto's bitcoin protocol. Bit gold combines various cryptographic and mining elements to achieve decentralization, including time-stamped blocks stored in a title registry and generated using proof-of-work (PoW) strings.

In the BitGold structure users have to solve cryptographic puzzles using computing power. It is then sent over the Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) peer-to-peer network and then assigned to the puzzle solver's public key. Each solution then becomes part of the next challenge and creates an ever-evolving chain of new properties, thereby validating blocks of transactions and stamping new coins. This is similar to the block generation process in bitcoin, where the hash address is used as a header pointing to the next set of blocks.

Szabo said he created a small amount of gold to address some inefficiencies in the traditional financial system. According to Szabo, parties have to invest a lot of trust for transactions to occur in the traditional financial system. Unfortunately, transacting through trust-based systems leaves consumers and financial institutions vulnerable to fraud or theft. When trying to design transactions with digital coins, you run into the “double spend problem.” Once the data is created, reproducing it is a simple matter of copying and pasting. Most digital currencies solve the problem by relinquishing some control to a central authority, which tracks the balance of each account. This is an unacceptable solution for Szabo.

“I try to replicate as closely as possible in cyberspace the security and trust characteristics of gold, and most importantly that it does not rely on a trusted central authority”

By Nick Szabo

⇒ B-money, Anonymous Distributed Electronic Cash System

B-money was an initial proposal made by Wei Dai for an "anonymous distributed electronic cash system". Satoshi Nakamoto referenced b-money when creating Bitcoin. At that time some people thought that Wei Dai was one of the Bitcoin developers and asked him several things about Bitcoin, then he replied:

“My understanding is that the creator of Bitcoin, Satoshi Nakamoto, didn't even read my article before reinventing the idea himself. He learned about it afterwards and praised me in his paper. So my connection to this project is very limited.”

By Wei Dai, in a comment on lesswrong.com2011/2/25

In his essay, published on the cypherpunks mailing list in November 1998, Dai proposed two protocols. The first protocol is impractical because it requires a broadcast channel that cannot be jammed and is also synchronous.

1. First protocol,
The use of the proof-of-work function is proposed as a means to make money. B-Money Dai was proposed in the context of a cypherpunks mailing list discussion relating to possible applications of Hashcash, the first symmetric proof-of-work function, which was also published on the same mailing list, the previous year - May 1997. (Like the B-money proposal, bitcoin itself was also uses hashcash fee function as proof-of-work during coin minting). In B-Money, money is transferred by broadcasting transactions to all participants, all of whom hold everyone else's accounts. Contracts may be made with the possibility of reparation in the event of default, with a third party agreeing to be the arbitrator. If there is no agreement, each party broadcasts arguments or evidence in its favor and each participant determines compensation/fines in his own account.

2. Second protocol,
The second protocol has only a subset of participants ("servers") keeping accounts, which they must make public, and participants making transactions verify their balances by querying many of them. The participants also verified that the money supply was not inflated. A certain amount of money as collateral is required to be a lost server if the server is found to be dishonest.

An alternative method for earning money is proposed, through an auction in which participants bid on the solution of a computing problem of known complexity.

⇒ Bitcoin, A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System

Bitcoin was first published on October 31 at 18.10 EDT 2008 on the site metzdown.com. The site has a mailing list where anyone who enters their email address can exchange information about cryptography. A person using the username Satoshi Nakamoto sent a short letter to the mailing list.

“I've been working on a new electronic cash system that is completely peer-to-peer, without trusted third parties.”

By Satoshi Nakamoto, metzdowd.com 2008/10/31

On August 18, 2008 the official Bitcoin website, namely Bitcoin.org, was born. The domain is registered at anonymousspeech.com, a site that allows users to register domain names anonymously. Bitcoin.org was originally registered and owned by two of Bitcoin's first developers, Satoshi Nakamoto and Martti Malmi. When Nakamoto left the project, he gave access rights to ownership of the domain to several people, but separate from the Bitcoin developers, to divide roles and responsibilities to prevent unilateral control by someone or a certain group of the Bitcoin project.

Bitcoin is a digital currency that can be sent online via the internet. Bitcoins are sent directly from one person to another via a peer-to-peer network. Unlike conventional fiat currencies, bitcoin is different in that there is no oversight that can control the value, transactions do not go through a third party and this makes costs much lower, people can use it in any country around the world, there will be no account freezes and there are no arbitrary requirements or limitations.

How does Bitcoin work :

  • Currency exchanges exist where you can buy and sell Bitcoin for Fiat Currency
  • Bitcoins are stored in a Digital Wallet on a Computer or Mobile Device
  • Sending Bitcoin is as easy as sending an E-mail
  • The Bitcoin network is protected by individuals called Miners
  • Miners are rewarded with newly generated Bitcoins for verifying transactions
  • All verified transactions will be recorded in a transparent public file

Transfers are facilitated directly without using a financial processor between nodes. This type of transaction makes chargebacks impossible. The Bitcoin client will broadcast the transaction to nearby nodes which will spread the payment across the network. Failed or incorrect transactions will be rejected by honest clients. Transactions are mostly free of charge, but a fee may usually be paid to other nodes to prioritize transaction processing.

Total circulation will eventually be 21 million bitcoins. There will never be more coins than that. Coins enter circulation gradually, at a steady rate over the years, to nodes supporting the network in proportion to the CPU time they contribute, it is estimated that by 2013 half of the total circulation will be successfully generated, and by 2017, 3/4 of it will be finished generating. As Bitcoin continues its journey to value, it will likely begin to experience deflation, with nodes in this period expected to rely on their ability to competitively collect transaction processing fees.

The term "Altcoin" is a combination of two words: "alt" and "coin" where alt means "alternative" and coin means "cryptocurrency". Together they imply the category of cryptocurrency, which is an alternative to the digital currency Bitcoin. After Bitcoin's success story, many other peer-to-peer digital currencies have emerged to try to replicate that success. Many altcoins are built on the basic structure provided by Bitcoin. Therefore, most altcoins are peer-to-peer, requiring a mining process where users solve the difficult problem of solving blocks and providing a safe and cheap way to conduct transactions online. But Altcoins, even with many overlapping features, vary greatly from each other.

Types of Altcoins :

  • Mining-Based, Most mining-based altcoins use Proof-of-Work (PoW), a method in which a system generates new coins by solving a difficult problem , to create blocks. Examples of mining-based Altcoins are Ethereum, Litecoin, Monero, and Zcash.
  • Stablecoins, Cryptocurrency trading and use has been characterized by volatility since its launch. Stablecoins aim to reduce this overall volatility by grouping their value into a basket of goods, such as fiat currency, precious metals, or other cryptocurrencies. Examples of Stablecoins are USDT, USDC, BUSD, TUSD, DAI and HUSD.
  • Security Tokens, Security tokens are similar to securities traded on stock markets except they have a digital provenance. Security tokens resemble traditional shares and often promise equity, in the form of ownership, or dividend payments to their holders. Security tokens are generally offered to investors through initial coin offerings (ICOs).
  • Meme Coins, Meme coins are inspired by jokes or silly views on other well-known cryptocurrencies. They usually gain popularity in a short period of time, often hyped online by prominent crypto influencers and retail investors trying to exploit short-term gains. For example, the CEO of Tesla, Inc. ( TSLA ) and cryptocurrency enthusiast Elon Musk regularly posts cryptic tweets about leading meme coins Dogecoin and Shiba Inu often substantially changing their prices.
  • Utility Tokens Utility tokens are used to provide services in the network. For example, they may be used to purchase services, pay network fees, or redeem rewards. Filecoin, which is used to purchase storage space on the network, is an example of a utility token.

Blind Signature

In cryptography a Blind Signature (Blind Signature), as introduced by David Chaum, is a form of digital signature in where the contents of the message are disguised (blind) before being signed. The resulting blind signature can be publicly verified against the original message that is not obfuscated by normal digital signature means. Blind signatures are commonly used in privacy-related protocols where the signer and author of the message are different parties. Examples include cryptographic voting systems and digital money schemes.
